If you are one of the many Australian parents who used TalkingParents because it was free, you have probably already noticed the change. As of 30 March 2026, TalkingParents requires a paid subscription to use the app. The free plan is gone.

For families who relied on it to keep co-parenting communication organised and on the record, this is a significant shift - and it raises an important question: is paying for a US-based app the best option for Australian families, or is there something better?

In this guide, we break down exactly what has changed, what TalkingParents now costs, and what alternatives are available - including options built specifically for Australian families.

## What happened to TalkingParents' free plan?

TalkingParents launched as a free co-parenting communication platform. For years, its free tier was one of its biggest selling points. Parents could exchange messages, share a calendar, and keep records without paying a cent.

That changed on 30 March 2026. TalkingParents now requires all users to subscribe to one of three paid plans. The company says the change allows them to "better support co-parents" with improved features, but the practical reality is that every parent now needs to pay.

TalkingParents does offer fee waivers for parents experiencing financial hardship or domestic violence, and a 30-day free trial for new users. But the days of genuinely free, ongoing access are over.

This follows a broader trend across the co-parenting app market. AppClose similarly removed its free tier in January 2026, moving to an all-paid model. The era of free co-parenting apps is winding down.

## Why TalkingParents is a poor fit for Australian families

Even before the pricing change, TalkingParents had significant limitations for Australian users. Now that it is a paid product, these gaps are harder to justify.

### 1. Your data is stored in the United States

TalkingParents is a US company that hosts all data on US servers. For Australian families, this means your private messages, calendar entries, expense records, and any documents you share are stored overseas.

This matters for two reasons. First, US-hosted data is subject to US law, including the Patriot Act and CLOUD Act, which allow US government agencies to access data without Australian legal oversight. Second, Australian courts may question the reliability and chain of custody of records held outside Australian jurisdiction.

CoRaise stores all data exclusively in Sydney, Australia (AWS ap-southeast-2 region), ensuring your information stays under Australian law at all times.

### 2. No Australian school terms or public holidays

TalkingParents' calendar does not include Australian school term dates, state-specific public holidays, or school holiday periods. If you are trying to plan parenting time around school terms - which is how most Australian parenting orders work - you are entering all of those dates manually.

CoRaise comes pre-loaded with [school term dates for every state and territory](/features), including state-specific public holidays like Melbourne Cup Day (VIC), the Royal Queensland Show (QLD), and Recreation Day (TAS). When term dates change, CoRaise updates them automatically.

### 3. No Evidence Act compliance

Australian courts operate under the *Evidence Act 1995* (Cth) and its state equivalents. For electronic records to be admissible, they need to meet specific reliability and authenticity requirements - including proof of who sent a message, when it was sent, and that the content has not been altered.

TalkingParents was built for US family courts, not Australian ones. Its records may still be accepted by an Australian court, but there is no guarantee they meet the specific requirements of the Evidence Act.

CoRaise generates SHA-256 content hashes for every message using database triggers at the time of insertion. This creates a tamper-proof chain of evidence. Our [court-admissible PDF exports](/features) include these verification hashes, making it straightforward for your lawyer to demonstrate authenticity under the Evidence Act.

### 4. Per-parent billing creates conflict

When each parent needs their own subscription, it creates an immediate question: who pays? In high-conflict co-parenting situations, billing disputes are the last thing you need.

CoRaise uses a single family subscription with optional [split billing](/pricing). One subscription covers both parents. If you choose to split the cost, each parent pays their portion directly - no arguments about who is paying for what.

### 5. USD pricing means unpredictable costs

Paying in USD means your actual cost changes with exchange rates. A plan that costs $122 USD today might cost more next month. Your bank may also charge international transaction fees of 1-3% on top.

CoRaise is priced in AUD. What you see is what you pay.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is TalkingParents completely free anymore?

No. As of 30 March 2026, all TalkingParents users need a paid subscription. The cheapest plan is the Essentials plan at $7 USD per month ($84 USD per year) per parent. Fee waivers are available for parents experiencing financial hardship or domestic violence, and there is a 30-day free trial for new users.

### Can I use TalkingParents records in an Australian court?

Potentially, but there is no guarantee. TalkingParents was designed for US courts and does not specifically comply with the Australian *Evidence Act 1995*. An Australian judge has discretion over whether to admit the records. Purpose-built Australian platforms like CoRaise generate SHA-256 verified records designed to meet Evidence Act requirements.

### How much does CoRaise cost compared to TalkingParents?

CoRaise costs $9.99 AUD per month for the Standard plan or $14.99 AUD per month for the Premium plan. This is a single family subscription that covers both parents. Many family lawyers are familiar with Australian-built platforms and can advise on the best approach for your situation.
