Choosing a co-parenting app after separation is one of those decisions that feels small but has real consequences. The app you pick will hold your private messages, your children's schedules, your financial records, and potentially evidence for court proceedings. It needs to work reliably, protect your privacy, and fit your budget.

The problem for Australian families is that most co-parenting apps are built for American users. They store data in the US, follow US legal standards, and include US school calendars. They price in USD and charge each parent separately.

In 2026, the landscape has shifted significantly. TalkingParents removed its free tier in March. AppClose went paid-only in January. Meanwhile, new Australian-built options have emerged. This guide covers every major co-parenting app available to Australian families right now, with honest assessments of each.

## What Australian families should look for in a co-parenting app

Before comparing individual apps, here is a checklist of features that matter specifically for Australian users. Most comparison articles skip these because they are written for US audiences.

### 1. Australian data hosting

Where your data is stored matters legally. Under the *Privacy Act 1988* (Cth), organisations that send personal information overseas must take reasonable steps to ensure the overseas recipient handles it in line with the Australian Privacy Principles. But enforcement is limited once data crosses borders.

More practically, if your co-parenting records end up in an Australian family court, having that data hosted in Australia under Australian law strengthens its evidentiary weight. US-hosted data is subject to the Patriot Act and CLOUD Act.

**What to look for**: Data stored in Australian data centres (ideally Sydney, ap-southeast-2).

### 2. Australian school terms and public holidays

Parenting orders in Australia almost always reference school terms. "The children spend the first half of each school holiday period with Parent A" is a standard clause. If your co-parenting app does not know when Australian school terms start and end - and those dates vary by state - you are doing unnecessary manual work.

**What to look for**: Pre-loaded school term dates for your state or territory, updated annually.

### 3. Evidence Act compliance

The *Evidence Act 1995* (Cth) and its state equivalents set out the rules for admitting electronic records in court. For co-parenting communication to be useful as evidence, you need to show who sent it, when, and that the content has not been tampered with.

**What to look for**: Content hashing (SHA-256 or similar), append-only records that cannot be edited or deleted, and court-ready PDF exports with verification data.

### 4. Family-based pricing

Most US apps charge per parent. That means both parents pay the full subscription fee separately. In a co-parenting context, this is a recipe for conflict - especially when one parent earns significantly more than the other.

**What to look for**: Per-family pricing where one subscription covers both parents, ideally with split billing so costs can be shared fairly.

### 5. Domestic violence safety features

One in four Australian women and one in thirteen Australian men have experienced violence from an intimate partner, according to the Australian Bureau of Statistics. A co-parenting app used by separated families must account for this reality.

**What to look for**: Emergency exit button, message cooling-off periods, harassment detection, block/restrict options, and notification privacy (no message previews).

### 6. Pricing in AUD

Paying in USD adds unpredictability. Exchange rates fluctuate, and most banks charge 1-3% in international transaction fees. AUD pricing means you know exactly what you are paying.

**What to look for**: Prices listed and charged in Australian dollars.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do both parents need to use the same app?

In most cases, yes. Co-parenting apps work by giving both parents access to a shared space for messaging, calendar, and expenses. If only one parent uses the app, there is no shared communication record.

If your parenting order requires communication through a specific platform, both parents are generally expected to use that platform.

### Can I use a free app instead?

As of 2026, genuinely free co-parenting apps with meaningful features are rare. TalkingParents and AppClose both removed their free tiers in early 2026. Some apps offer free trials (AppClose offers 60 days, 2Houses offers 14 days), and most offer fee waivers for financial hardship.

CoRaise offers a free trial period, and its Standard plan at $9.99 AUD per month covers both parents - making it approximately $5 per parent per month.

### Will Australian courts accept records from a US-based app?

Australian courts have discretion over what evidence to admit. Records from US-based apps may be accepted, but the judge will consider the reliability and authenticity of the records. Australian-hosted records with content verification (like SHA-256 hashing) are likely to carry more weight under the *Evidence Act 1995*.

### What if my co-parent will not switch apps?

This is common. If there is no court order specifying a particular app, you can suggest the switch and explain the benefits. If your co-parent refuses, you may need to continue using the current platform or seek a court order. A family lawyer can advise on the best approach for your situation.

### Can I export my data from another app to CoRaise?

CoRaise does not currently offer a data import tool from other platforms. The recommended approach is to export and save your records from your current platform, then use CoRaise for all future communication. Your historical records remain valid as a separate archive.

### What about just using email or text messages?

Email and text messages can work for low-conflict co-parenting, but they have significant drawbacks. Messages can be deleted, screenshots can be manipulated, and there is no shared calendar or expense tracking. If there is any chance your communication might be needed in court, a purpose-built co-parenting app provides much stronger evidence.
